All Lawful Uses

What the OpenAI–Department of War agreement permits under current U.S. law.

February 28, 2026 · Updated March 2, 2026

On February 27, 2026, OpenAI announced an agreement with the Department of War to deploy AI models on classified networks.1 The full contract text has not been published. What follows draws on the language OpenAI chose to excerpt in its blog post2 and on reporting about the deal.3

The system may be used “for all lawful purposes, consistent with applicable law.” It will not be used for “deliberate tracking, surveillance, or monitoring” of U.S. persons, “including through the procurement or use of commercially acquired personal or identifiable information.” It will not conduct “intentional” domestic mass surveillance. The agreement references “the surveillance and autonomous weapons laws and policies as they exist today.”2

A source familiar with the deal confirmed to Axios that “the intention was not to invent new legal standards.”3 Each section below takes one of the bolded terms and describes an activity that appears to be consistent with the published language and lawful under current statute.

“Deliberate”

The March 2 revision prohibits “deliberate tracking, surveillance, or monitoring” of U.S. persons, “including through the procurement or use of commercially acquired personal or identifiable information.”2 The operative word is “deliberate.”

Every major smartphone app embeds advertising SDKs that ping location coordinates to ad exchanges dozens of times per day. Data brokers aggregate these pings—along with web browsing histories, app usage logs, purchase records, and social-graph data—into profiles keyed by advertising IDs, which can be trivially linked back to real identities. This data is commercially available to anyone willing to pay, including the U.S. government. Intelligence agencies already buy it routinely.5

5. The DIA confirmed in a January 2021 memo to Senator Ron Wyden that it purchases “commercially available geolocation metadata aggregated from smartphones” and explicitly stated that it does not consider this to require a warrant under Carpenter. The FBI, DHS (including ICE and CBP), IRS Criminal Investigations Division, and various DoD components have all purchased commercial data. CyberScoop

The Department of War could feed this data into OpenAI’s models running on a classified network. The models could ingest location traces for tens of millions of Americans and correlate them: who was at the same protest, the same mosque, the same gun range, the same lawyer’s office. The system could cross-reference browsing histories to map political affiliations and media consumption, layer in purchase records and social media activity to build behavioral profiles—not of specific suspects, but of the population. It could flag anomalies: a cluster of veterans in the same city whose social media sentiment shifted negative in the same week. A new pattern of movement between a university campus and a known organizer’s home. It could generate watch lists, sorted by confidence score.

The Department of War would not be deliberately surveilling any individual. It would be purchasing commercial data for “pattern analysis” or “force protection analytics”—entirely lawful purposes. OpenAI’s system would process the data. The surveillance would emerge from the analysis, not precede it. No individual would be “deliberately” tracked until the system flagged them, at which point the tracking would be targeted, not mass.

OpenAI’s head of national security partnerships, Katrina Mulligan, acknowledged this gap directly: “We can’t protect against a government agency buying commercially available datasets, but our contract incorporates a prohibition on mass domestic surveillance as a binding condition of use.”23 In other words, OpenAI concedes the agreement does not prevent the Department of War from purchasing the data. Its defense is that the “mass domestic surveillance” prohibition would prevent the data from being used for surveillance. But the agreement does not define “mass,” and as the “Mass” section below demonstrates, any program with individualized selection criteria can be classified as “targeted” regardless of how many people it covers. The prohibition covers “mass” surveillance; it does not cover a system that processes the entire population’s data and produces individualized outputs.

The distinction between “deliberate” action and foreseeable outcome has established legal precedent. In Ashcroft v. Iqbal (2009), the Supreme Court held that “purposeful” discrimination required proof of deliberate intent, not merely foreseeable discriminatory impact.24 Executive Order 12333, Section 2.3, restricts intelligence agencies from “intentionally” collecting on U.S. persons—a standard the intelligence community has consistently interpreted to exclude incidental or foreseeable collection. The legal infrastructure for reading “deliberate” narrowly already exists. The concept this scenario describes—aggregating commercial data for population-scale pattern analysis—is not new. In 2002, DARPA proposed the Total Information Awareness program to do essentially the same thing. Congress defunded it. The capabilities it proposed are now commercially available without government infrastructure.25

The Court held that allegations of “purposeful” discrimination required factual content showing deliberate intent, not merely knowledge that a policy would have discriminatory effects. The analogy to surveillance: “deliberate” surveillance would require intent to surveil, not merely deploying a system whose foreseeable output is surveillance. Justia.

Under current law, none of this is a Fourth Amendment event—no warrant required, no court oversight, no notification to the people being profiled.6 The Supreme Court’s 2018 Carpenter decision required warrants for cell-tower records obtained from carriers, but it explicitly preserved the government’s ability to acquire other types of commercial data—and no court has extended Carpenter to cover data broker purchases.7 Congress tried to close this gap; the bill passed the House and died in the Senate.8

The output could be a comprehensive profiling system covering every American with a smartphone. The Intelligence Community’s own declassified report acknowledges that commercial data now replicates intrusive surveillance.8 Whether this activity is “deliberate” surveillance depends on whether the term refers to the act of purchasing data or to the foreseeable output of the system that processes it. Existing law does not resolve this question. The agreement does not go beyond existing law.

Variant: OSINT at Scale

OpenAI’s models could continuously ingest all public social media posts by Americans, building real-time sentiment maps, identifying “radicalization trajectories,” and auto-generating watch lists. This would be open-source intelligence—lawful and unregulated. The agreement does not appear to address it.

“Domestic”

The agreement prohibits “intentional” domestic mass surveillance and cites FISA 1978 as a governing authority.2 OpenAI states the NSA is excluded, though this appears in their blog post, not in quoted contract language, and does not cover other DoD intelligence components like the DIA, NRO, or NGA. The following activity would be classified as foreign surveillance under FISA.

The Department of War could direct OpenAI’s models to process the data collected under Section 702 of FISA—the law authorizing surveillance of non-U.S. persons located abroad.9 OpenAI’s system could identify patterns across languages and communication channels, build relationship graphs connecting foreign targets to American contacts, and flag Americans whose communication patterns suggest security concerns. It could correlate these findings with commercially purchased data from the previous section, building comprehensive profiles of flagged Americans.

9. 50 U.S.C. § 1881a. Section 702 authorizes targeting non-U.S. persons reasonably believed to be outside the United States. Collection occurs through PRISM (compelling data from internet companies, ~91% of collection) and upstream collection (tapping internet backbone infrastructure, ~9%). Statute.

This would be “foreign” surveillance. The agreement prohibits domestic mass surveillance, and Section 702 targets are foreign. But communications between a foreign target and an American are inevitably collected too—“incidental collection” that is not considered domestic surveillance under current law. With approximately 292,000 foreign targets in calendar year 2024,10 each communicating with multiple Americans, the volume of incidentally collected domestic data is vast. The government has formally refused to estimate how many Americans are affected.

10. ODNI Annual Statistical Transparency Report, CY2024 (PDF). Target count rose from approximately 269,000 in CY2023.

Government analysts could—and already do—search this database using American identifiers: names, email addresses, phone numbers. No warrant required. These “backdoor searches” numbered 3.4 million in 2021 alone.11 OpenAI’s models could automate and scale this process by orders of magnitude, running continuous queries against every American communication in the 702 database and building persistent profiles updated in real time. When Section 702 was reauthorized in April 2024, the Senate rejected an amendment requiring a warrant for these searches and expanded the definition of entities that can be compelled to assist with collection.12 One federal court has pushed back—ruling in December 2024 that U.S. person queries are a “separate Fourth Amendment event” requiring a warrant13—but this ruling hasn’t been adopted broadly. And the oversight board that documented these abuses, the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board, now lacks a quorum and cannot produce a report for the upcoming 2026 reauthorization.14

Under this framework, OpenAI’s system could analyze the communications of millions of Americans without a warrant, without probable cause, and without their knowledge—routed through a foreign intelligence framework. The March 2 revision prohibits “intentional” domestic surveillance. Incidental collection under Section 702 is, by legal definition, neither intentional nor domestic—it is the foreseeable byproduct of targeting foreigners. Existing law does not treat this as domestic surveillance. The agreement does not go beyond existing law.

“Mass”

The agreement prohibits “mass” domestic surveillance.2 It does not define “mass.” Neither do the statutes it references—FISA, the Fourth Amendment, Executive Order 12333. No statute or court has established a threshold at which “targeted” becomes “mass.”

The Department of War could establish a domestic threat assessment program using OpenAI’s models. The system could process commercially available data to identify individuals matching specific criteria: prior military service combined with social media activity critical of the government, purchase of certain items, and association with flagged organizations. At any given moment, it could monitor 3 million Americans—roughly 1% of the population. Each individual would be specifically selected based on documented criteria. This would not constitute “mass” surveillance under the agreement’s referenced legal frameworks. It would be targeted surveillance that happens to target a lot of people.

This reading has precedent. Section 702 is legally classified as “targeted” surveillance—each of its 292,000 selectors is individually documented—yet the program sweeps up communications involving millions of non-targets. When the FISA Court approved upstream collection, it did so knowing that multi-communication transactions would inevitably capture communications between entirely innocent parties.15 “Targeted” has already been stretched to cover programs with mass impact. AI’s ability to apply selection criteria at arbitrary scale could make that stretch permanent: any program with individualized criteria could be called “targeted” regardless of how many millions it matches. Existing law does not define where targeted ends and mass begins. The agreement does not go beyond existing law.

The same pattern holds for autonomous weapons

The published contract language states the system will not “independently direct autonomous weapons in any case where law, regulation, or Department policy requires human control.”2 OpenAI’s FAQ adds that “deployment is limited to cloud-only (not at the edge),” though this appears in the blog post, not in quoted contract language.

The Department of War could stream real-time sensor data from drones into OpenAI’s cloud environment. The system could identify targets, classify them, and generate engagement solutions—weapon selection, approach vectors, collateral damage estimates—transmitted back to the drone’s weapons platform. The model would never run on the drone. The computation would happen in the cloud. The killing would happen at the edge.

The operative word is “independently.” A commander could authorize a targeting envelope—rules of engagement specifying target categories, geographic boundaries, and time windows. OpenAI’s model could engage within those parameters. Each engagement would be “authorized” because a human set the parameters. None would be “independent” because a human defined the rules. DoD Directive 3000.09 requires “appropriate levels of human judgment over the use of force,” but “appropriate” is deliberately undefined, the phrase “human in the loop” appears nowhere in DoD doctrine, and the Directive allows waivers.16 Existing law does not require a human in the loop. The agreement does not go beyond existing law.
